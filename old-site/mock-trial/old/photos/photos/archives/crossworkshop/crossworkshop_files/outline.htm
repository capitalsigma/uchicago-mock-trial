Startle with Silence: The Pin-Drop Effect
No Substitute for Preparation
Plan and re-plan the sequence of your questions
Every witness should have a cross-examination sheet that includes:
What you hope to accomplish
Factual building blocks needed to accomplish your goal
The factual building blocks must have specific citations to an affidavit, deposition or exhibit
Watch out for the Judge
Do not anger the judge
Make non-responsive objections in accordance with the judge’s prior rulings
Do not react to rulings
If the judge rules in your favor do not seem happy or relieved
If the judge rules against you do not seem upset or annoyed
Exercise Self-Control
No matter what the witness says, Never show surprise at any answer
You should always appear like the answer that was given was the answer that you expected
Always take a damaging answer as a matter of course and let it fall flat
Avoid asking one question too many
Never argue during cross-examination
Lay out the facts and admissions that you need for your argument
Never attempt to get the witness to agree with your characterization of a certain situation
Never ask any witness to explain anything ever!
No attorney should ever utter the words “How” or “Why” on cross-examination
If you lay out your questions correctly, the facts you elicit will argue for you
Do not be indignant
“It is all right for you once in a while to act indignant. But never be indignant”
-Judge Learned Hand
Never get angry with any witness no matter what they do
Letting your anger show will only hurt your score on cross-examination
It is much better to proceed calmly and respectfully in establishing how the witness has “misbehaved”
Consider timing
Always look for a high point to end you cross-examination on
Even if you haven’t finished all of your questions a damaging admission can be better to end on especially in mock trial
Plan your sequence to put your strongest point last in you examination
Your final point should be impossible for the witness to disagree with (I.e. a direct quote from their affidavit)
Avoid flip remarks & petty points
Never make a comment after an answer to a question
Never say “I see…,” “Interesting,” “Exactly,” “Hmmm…” or “Thank You”
Do not go after trivial inconsistencies that came out on direct
Only impeach if the contradiction is obvious and important for your case
Only bring up negative personal characteristics of the witness if it is relevant to your case
Startle with silence: The Pin-Drop Effect
When the witness makes a material admission during cross, stop asking questions for a moment to let the answer sink in.
After you ask your last cross question, which will hopefully be your strongest point, always pause at least three seconds before saying “No further questions”
Some last remarks
Your questions should be pithy, uncharacterized, and closed-ended
Be yourself
Facts, facts, facts
When you have made your point sit down!
Cross-Examination in Mock Trial
How to Score Well
How to accomplish these things:
Stick to a few important points
Keep your questions tightly worded and leading
Don’t make big jumps in questions
Order questions carefully to make an argument implicit
DON’T ASK ONE QUESTION TOO MANY!!!
Differences between Mock Trial and real trial
Emphasis on showmanship
Witness is trying to fight you
Judged more on presentation then on content
What the above means:
Looking confide*ant is more important than eliciting vital information
Witness control is essential
Approaching Different Witness Types
Sobbing Widow
compassionate & level tone, firm yet not overbearing, sympathy
Defendant
varies, but usually be very firm, watch for invention/impeachment points, aggressive if situation warrants
Eye Witness
pin them to exactly what they saw, may have to be careful if eye witness is very emotional
Respectable Expert
ask technical expert-like questions, challenge expertise on issue, cross on background (usually some mess-up in past)
Peculiar Expert
ask technical expert-like questions & question witness' expertise even more, cross on background (always some previous mess-up)
Impartial fact witness
pin them to their affidavit, might also have to be careful if witness is too emotional
Kooky fact witness
usually can attack methods, character or credibility
Your Checklist for trial
Watch the Judge
listen to their responses to objections,
whether they limit a witness' answers
how they react to certain themes presented
how they react to different witness types
Watch the Witness
How they portray their witnesses
often, witnesses are portrayed in similar fashions across the board
a team’s witnesses will probably fight on cross, all offer additional information on their responses, and all invent (to some extent)
If one witness does it, it's likely that they all do!
Watch for a theme
if their witnesses are concentrating on one aspect of the case try to avoid asking questions that allow them to emphasize their theme
Grill them on the main issues and sit down
Watch the Attorney
generally, a team is better at some things than others i.e. They may use relevance or foundation objections very well
so be ready to defend yourself on those pieces of evidence that are only partially relevant, etc.
